Designing Google Calendar for iOS

Connor Gramazio

Some of the most frustrating aspects of being a PhD student — and other occupations — is keeping track of long-term deadlines. Last minute sprints to get a deliverable out the door are always hard to avoid, but are even harder if you are unable to maintain mindfulness about distant deadlines. With the popularity of smartphones, many people rely on mobile solutions for task management; but I have yet to find an app-based solution that satisfactorily targets long-term planning.

With these ideas in mind I've designed a mock interface for what Google Calendar might look like on iOS. The interface is based on the look and feel of Google's current iOS applications and adds in a conceptual feature where users can automagically mark long-term deadlines. Design details and motivation

Although there are some great calendar applications (I'm a huge fan of Sunrise), it would be nice to have 1st-party support from Google. Likewise, there are already droves of task management tools (e.g., Omnifocus, Things, Any.DO, and Wunderlist). But these tools back-seat long-term tasks, in favor of short-term tasks. This isn't ideal for users in academia, where long-term tasks run our day-to-day organization and planning.

One great way to help promote mindfulness is to have reminders about long-term deadlines in places you'll check often. Almost everyone creates a calendar event six months down the line that says "[Deliverable] Deadline," and then promptly forgets its existence until you are in a work frenzy the week of. My design focuses on making a calendar application first and foremost, but one structured around providing at-a-glance, long-term reminders.



The long-term reminders sit at the top of the screen as three bright red boxes. The mosts saturated red shows the most urgent long-term deadline, while less crucial deadlines receive a successively less-saturated red. From the color selection for the other parts of the application, this red is more salient and therefore a more prominent feature. This assures that while it still takes up a small amount of screen real estate it will not be forgotten.

Another important component is the ability to add and manage long-term deadlines. While the application should intelligently decide on a todo ordering, users should still be able to configure what they want shown and what they want to have considered a long-term deadline. To do this users would have the ability to toggle a flag during event creation (or through editing an existing event) that adds it to the long-term deadline list. Once users have a list of long-term deadlines they can edit the ordering by tapping the three long-term deadline bars on the top of the application's home screen. This provides a detail-view for the three deadlines and shows detail-views of lower priority deadlines. Each deadline can be reordered by dragging a box using the anchoring lines on the right side of each deadline's box.



Testing effectiveness

Being an HCI-minded PhD student, when designing the interface I started to think about how I could test if my conceptual deadline tracking feature would actually make a difference. My hypothesis is that having an at-a-glance deadline tracker in a calendar application would make a positive difference. I think the best option to actually test this would be to do a field study similar to the field studies performed by Consolvo et al. that loked at a phsyical activity tracking tool. The primary novelty of the application was an at-a-glance display of how active a given subject was through a garden display (the more active, the better the garden). In their work, Consolvo et al. gave a tracking tool to twelve subjects for three weeks and were able to look at changes in exercise as well as collect self-reported insight into how satisfied users were with the fitness application.

An evaluation of my proposed Google Calendar app could use a similar methodology, where we would gather a population of users who have never used todo tracking software (e.g., OmniFocus) but who are interested in such a service. We would also gather a population who has prior experience using mobile todo software. Then, over the course of a half-year we ask each subject to use the todo software and give regular reports through an online survey of how mindful they were about upcoming deadlines.

Ideally, the prior-use population would evenly consist of people who have used a variety of todo-tracking software with and without calendars. The primary research question would be whether the at-a-glance, limited-view of far away deadlines makes mindfulness easier, and whether such mindfulness translates into better time management.
